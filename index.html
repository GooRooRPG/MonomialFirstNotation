Monomial-First Notation v4.0

Monomial First Notation (MFN) is a proposed standardization of mathematical grammar designed to eliminate the ambiguity found in standard operational precedence (PEMDAS/BODMAS). By defining parentheses and juxtaposition as explicit binary operations with the highest priority, MFN transforms mathematical expressions from linear strings of instructions into a predictable hierarchy of "Monomial Atoms. "

Mathematical notation, as commonly written in infix form, relies on procedural conventions such as order of operations (PEMDAS) to resolve ambiguity. These rules—prioritizing parentheses, exponents, multiplication/division, and addition/subtraction—emerged historically as a way to linearize inherently hierarchical structures. While effective, they introduce ambiguity in edge cases, particularly when implicit multiplication (e.g., adjacency) interacts with explicit operators. Expressions such as 6/2(3) illustrate this tension, as their interpretation depends on convention rather than structure.

MFN proposes a structural alternative. Instead of treating expressions as linear strings interpreted through precedence rules, MFN treats them as compositions of monomials—each a cohesive unit formed through adjacency and exponentiation. Explicit operators act only as separators between these units. In this framework, expressions are represented as a hierarchical structure rather than a sequence.

Computation in MFN is defined as a process of structural reduction over this hierarchy. Unlike Reverse Polish Notation (RPN), which encodes a fixed sequence of operations, the system evolves through local transformations toward irreducible forms. This eliminates reliance on procedural precedence rules and replaces them with explicit structural relationships, yielding a representation that is both unambiguous and lossless with respect to its original form.

The proposed framework is suitable for educational tools, deterministic calculators, parser design, and symbolic expression systems. MFN reinterprets parenthesized adjacency as a formal binary operation with implied identity shorthand. MFN preserves familiar notation while producing deterministic parse trees and removing common ambiguities. It offers a bridge between traditional arithmetic notation and modern parser-oriented computation systems.

Another key difference is we treat parentheses not as simple grouping symbol but as a marker for a factor of a monomial. Note this does not apply to mathematical functions like sin(x). Every parentheses or algebraic variable gets a binary P node in the tree. P(a,b) = a*b. M(a,b) = a*b. There are two nodes that both map to multiplication but P is used for multiplying internal factors of a monomial, and M is used for multiplying two separate monomials. This creates a spine structure with a leading numeric coefficient, and a sequence of factors each with their own units and exponents. MFN allows for round trip conversion between linear infix notation, and internal tree structure with no ambiguity. MFN is able to differentiate between x and (x), which most systems don't since they immediately throw away the parentheses. This is interpreted as a variable x, inside of another variable (the parentheses) and results in two P nodes in the tree. Fully expanded it is 1(1x^1)^1.

MFN proves that linearity does not have to mean ambiguity. If we define a Monomial as an unbreakable recursive chain (an Atom), then the "Order of Operations" is simplified. Complete the Atom before you interact with it. We don't need fractions or complex formatting to be clear. We just need to stop treating math as a list of actions and start treating it as a description of objects. MFN keeps the convenience Cajori wanted, but restores the structural honesty that Lennes fought for. This reconciles the two sides of the 100-year war.
